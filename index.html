1. Units: The Language of Measurement

Units are fundamental in physics; they give meaning to numbers. Without units, a measurement is just a number without context. This section will guide you through SI units, common prefixes, and an interactive converter to practice conversions.

Key Concepts: SI Base & Derived Units

SI Base Units

These are the fundamental building blocks of all measurements.

  • Length: meter (m)
  • Mass: kilogram (kg)
  • Time: second (s)
  • Electric Current: ampere (A)
  • Temperature: Kelvin (K)
  • Amount of Substance: mole (mol)
  • Luminous Intensity: candela (cd)

Derived Units

Units formed by combining base SI units.

  • Speed: meters per second (m/s)
  • Force: Newton (N), which is kg·m/s²
  • Energy: Joule (J), which is kg·m²/s²
  • Power: Watt (W), which is J/s or kg·m²/s³
  • Pressure: Pascal (Pa), which is N/m² or kg/(m·s²)

SI Prefixes: Scaling Units

Prefixes are used to denote multiples or submultiples of units, making it easier to express very large or very small quantities. Understanding these is key to unit conversion.

2. Dimensions: The Nature of Physical Quantities

Dimensions refer to the fundamental physical nature of a quantity (e.g., length, mass, time), independent of the units used. Dimensional analysis is a powerful technique for checking the consistency of equations and even deriving relationships between quantities.

Key Concepts: Dimensional Formula & Homogeneity

Dimensional Formula

An expression showing how a derived quantity depends on the fundamental quantities. We use square brackets: [M] for mass, [L] for length, [T] for time.

Example: Dimensions of Speed = [L][T]⁻¹

Principle of Homogeneity of Dimensions

An equation is dimensionally correct if the dimensions of all terms on both sides of the equation are the same. This is a crucial check for any physics formula.

Example: In s = ut + ½at², all terms must have dimensions of [L].

Step-by-Step Dimensional Analysis Examples

Example 1: Dimensions of Force

Formula: Force = Mass × Acceleration

  1. Identify Base Dimensions: Mass is [M].
  2. Break Down Derived Quantities:
    • Velocity = Distance / Time = [L]/[T] = [L][T]⁻¹
    • Acceleration = Velocity / Time = [L][T]⁻¹ / [T] = [L][T]⁻²
  3. Substitute into Formula: Dimensions of Force = [M] × [L][T]⁻²
  4. Simplify: Dimensions of Force = [M][L][T]⁻²

Example 2: Dimensions of Work/Energy

Formula: Work = Force × Displacement

  1. Identify Base Dimensions: Displacement is [L].
  2. Use Known Derived Dimensions: Force = [M][L][T]⁻² (from Example 1).
  3. Substitute into Formula: Dimensions of Work = [M][L][T]⁻² × [L]
  4. Simplify: Dimensions of Work = [M][L]²[T]⁻²

Note: Energy has the same dimensions as Work, consistent with E = ½mv² where m is [M] and v² is ([L][T]⁻¹)² = [L]²[T]⁻². So, [M][L]²[T]⁻².

SOH CAH TOA: Finding Vector Components

SOH CAH TOA is a mnemonic for the definitions of the three basic trigonometric functions: Sine, Cosine, and Tangent. These are essential for breaking down vectors into their X and Y components, or finding a vector's angle and magnitude from its components.

The Rules

  • **SOH**: Sine(\theta) = Opposite / Hypotenuse
  • **CAH**: Cosine(\theta) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
  • **TOA**: Tangent(\theta) = Opposite / Adjacent

For a vector \vec{A} with magnitude A and angle \theta from the positive x-axis:

  • A_x = A \cos \theta (Adjacent)
  • A_y = A \sin \theta (Opposite)
  • A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2} (Hypotenuse)
  • \theta = \arctan(A_y / A_x)

4. Essential Physics Formulas for Dimensional Analysis

This section provides a quick reference for common physics formulas. Understanding these formulas and the quantities they relate is crucial for performing dimensional analysis and checking the consistency of equations. Pay attention to the variables and their corresponding physical quantities.

Kinematics & Dynamics

  • **Displacement:** s = ut + ½at²
    ($s$: displacement, $u$: initial velocity, $t$: time, $a$: acceleration)
  • **Velocity:** v = u + at
    ($v$: final velocity)
  • **Force (Newton's 2nd Law):** F = ma
    ($F$: force, $m$: mass, $a$: acceleration)
  • **Kinetic Energy:** KE = ½mv²
    ($KE$: kinetic energy, $m$: mass, $v$: velocity)
  • **Work Done:** W = Fd \cos \theta
    ($W$: work, $F$: force, $d$: displacement, $\theta$: angle)
  • **Power:** P = W/t or P = Fv
    ($P$: power, $W$: work, $t$: time, $F$: force, $v$: velocity)

Other Key Formulas

  • **Density:** \rho = m/V
    ($\rho$: density, $m$: mass, $V$: volume)
  • **Pressure:** P = F/A
    ($P$: pressure, $F$: force, $A$: area)
  • **Momentum:** p = mv
    ($p$: momentum, $m$: mass, $v$: velocity)
  • **Impulse:** J = F\Delta t
    ($J$: impulse, $F$: force, $\Delta t$: change in time)
  • **Frequency:** f = 1/T
    ($f$: frequency, $T$: period)
  • **Angular Velocity:** \omega = \Delta \theta / \Delta t
    ($\omega$: angular velocity, $\Delta \theta$: angular displacement, $\Delta t$: time)
